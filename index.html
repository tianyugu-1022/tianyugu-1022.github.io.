What is depression?

Depression is a common and serious mental health condition that affects how a person feels, thinks, and handles daily activities. It involves persistent feelings of sadness or a lack of interest or pleasure in previously enjoyable activities. Depression is different from normal mood fluctuations, such as feeling down or having a bad day, and can impact overall well-being and functioning. It can occur due to a combination of genetic, biological, environmental, and psychological factors. People experiencing depressive symptoms should seek professional help.

How Depression is Diagnosed?

Depression is diagnosed by a mental health professional (like a psychologist, psychiatrist, or licensed therapist) based on specific criteria found in diagnostic tools like the DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition). To be diagnosed, a person must experience certain core symptoms for at least two weeks. These symptoms must cause significant distress or impair daily functioning (work, school, relationships, etc.). Major Symptoms of Depression

A person may be diagnosed with Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) if they experience five or more of the following symptoms nearly every day for at least two weeks, and at least one of the symptoms must be either:

  • Depressed mood (feeling sad, empty, hopeless)
  • Loss of interest or pleasure in most activities (anhedonia)
  • Significant changes in appetite or weight
  • Sleep disturbances (insomnia or oversleeping)
  • Feeling restless or physically slowed down (psychomotor agitation or retardation)
  • Fatigue or low energy
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
  • Trouble with concentrating or decision-making
  • Thoughts of death or suicidal ideation
